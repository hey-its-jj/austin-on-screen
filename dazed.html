A group of teenagers run around getting drunk, flirting and pulling pranks on each other over the course of a summer night in 1976. That’s Dazed and Confused. It’s one of those movies that feels more like a crazy night out than a rigidly structured narrative—like The Warriors, or Night Shift or, more recently, Mid90s. It’s a coming-of-age film that follows a handful of characters as they all confront their place among their peers and among society. There’s not so much a single main character; it’s more like a common experience.

IN THREE WORDS...

This movie is stylish, structureless, and safe

Okay, so I like Dazed and Confused. I appreciate that it dedicates itself to setting the scene and not necessarily to punching you in the gut like so many coming-of-age movies do. Here’s the rub, though: All it has to offer is the setting. You saw how much I struggled to describe this movie. There’s not a lot of meat beneath the nostalgic veneer, polished as it is. It’s almost like it’s trying to tap into the value of vintage home video without the benefit of the authentic experience. What little drama there is tends to be pretty cliched.

To make matters worse, there’s another coming-of-age dramedy set in the 70s that I like way more, and it’s called Almost Famous. I don’t want to hijack Dazed and Confused’s spotlight, so I’ll just leave it at that. Almost Famous offers the same thing and more, because it has an actual plot that’s kind of interesting. Dazed and Confused is fascinating as a kind of manufactured porthole into the 70s, but it might not keep your attention.
