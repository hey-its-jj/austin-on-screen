Peter Gibbons, white-collar schmuck, undergoes occupational hypnotherapy in an attempt to assuage the existential dread inherent to his soul-sucking nine-to-five. To Peter’s surprise, it works, awakening within him what could easily be the latent ghost of a hedonist stoner. Peter’s new lackadaisical philosophy, as well as an increasingly turgid docket of workplace injustices, works to turn the corporate pecking order topsy-turvy.

Office Space was directed by Mike Judge, creator of Beavis & Butthead and King of the Hill. It didn’t do great at the box office, but it’s certainly garnered its own cult appreciation, and it stands as an eerily accurate satire of cubical culture even twenty-plus years later. I can say that because I worked a job just like Peter’s for two years, and I’ve been depressed ever since.

THE LOCATIONS

I was stoked to find out that Office Space was filmed in Austin. I was nervous about the locations for this one because I thought some businessowner would come out yelling at us to leave. I spent the nights following our outing agonizing over contigency plans and alibis, but it turns out we got the footage we needed without any issues.

IN THREE WORDS...

This movie is clever, fun, and simple

Office Space is short, funny, endlessly quotable and, above all, still relevant. I feel like every comedy ever has been described in exactly that way, but I’m creatively bankrupt, so just take my word for it. Mike Judge tends to demonstrate a very personal understanding of the people and situations he writes about. The characters themselves are a little one-note, but the dialogue is really where the fun is at. Peter’s romance with Joanna feels a bit shallow, but this isn’t a romcom. The soundtrack goes crazy; a good handful of nostalgic g-hop tracks to complement the absurd dichotomy between g-shit and office culture. There’s no caveat there. I know I kind of set it up like there would be, but the soundtrack is just cool.

Anyway, Office Space isn’t going to blow your mind, but you know what? It doesn’t have to. I appreciate a movie that you can watch on a whim, without thinking about whether you have the time or are in the mood. That’s not to say that this film is superficial; it has something to say, but it’s still light and fun. Digestible, I guess.
