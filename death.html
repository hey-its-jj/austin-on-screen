Depraved killer “Stuntman Mike” preys on women from behind the wheel of his vintage muscle car. His murderous streak hits a bump in the road when he targets a group of women who are determined to take him down, and poetically at that. Directed by Quentin Tarantino, Death Proof is—from what I’ve read—an homage to the gnarly grindhouse cinema of the 1970s: visceral, action-packed, profane and grungy, with somewhat of a patchwork quality about it. It’s set, at least partially, in Austin, TX—specifically along South Congress.

THE LOCATIONS

I was stressed out about getting the Guero's footage. South Congress is a bustling-ass street. Parking was a nightmare, not just because it was hard to find, but because paying for it set me back FIFTEEN clams, and we weren't even there for ten minutes. And the intersections are so confusing downtown! It sucked.

IN THREE WORDS...

This movie is slow, vulgar, and disappointing

Full disclosure: I couldn’t finish Death Proof. I only watched until about forty minutes in. The entire first hour is essentially a prologue, which itself is essentially a compilation of boring, repetitive and lengthy conversations about debauched sex capped off with a weird lap dance in the middle of a chili bar. It’s a departure from most of Tarantino’s other films, which tend to move a mile a minute. I’m not kidding; if you HATE talk-y movies, steer clear. Even if you’re curious like I was, there’s nothing here for you, at least not in the first sixty-or-so minutes.

So Death Proof is meandering and gratuitous in many aspects, even considering Tarantino’s admiration for grindhouse. Can’t recommend. Those nachos at Guero’s look pretty good, though.
